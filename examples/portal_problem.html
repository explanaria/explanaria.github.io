Portals and Conservation of Energy

I've been thinking about how to mathematically formalize portals in such a way that energy is conserved. I'll illustrate it here with a thought experiment.
Let's say we have two portals hovering above earth.
Anything that goes into one portal should come out of the same spot on the other one, maintaining its velocity. Ideally, space should be smoothly connected - each neighborhood looks Euclidean.
(except perhaps the portal's edges, which I'm ignoring for now.)
Assume we're in space, above earth. Newtonian gravity says there should be a force downwards, calculated from the distance r between a particle and the earth.
The particle should follow the gradient of gravitational potential.
Notice how because of the portals, this acceleration can keep accumularing for an infinitely long time, so the particle can gain infinite amounts of energy. Obviously, this violates the conservation of energy.

But why is the particle able to attain infinite energy? Usually, gravity is a conservative force: potential energy is the same no matter the path one takes to get from A to B. But consider two points, each ε away from a portal. Through the portal, they're only a tiny distance of 2ε away from one another, so they should feel the same force of gravity.

Newtonian gravity says the force F is proportional to 1/r2, where r is the distance from the point to Earth. Therefore, since the top point is farther from Earth, Newtonian physics says it experiences less gravity. Can we change how we define r to fix the problem?

Let's allow gravity to flow through portals. We can do this by defining the force of gravity as k/r2 again; but this time, let's define r as the length of shortest path to Earth, and make the resulting force the vector sum of all possible paths. This seems to fix the problem where a particle could falling infinitely long - anything near the top portal would feel a force of gravity upwards through that portal.

Interestingly, this also means that it's possible to exactly balance a point in gravity in such a way that the force of gravity exactly balances itself out out, meaning a point can hover there forever untill it's perturbed.

This vector summation rule also accounts for paths that take multiple trips through the portal! Let d be the distance between the two portals. If a path loops through the portals n times, each time, the resulting distance r is increased by d. The resulting force is the infinite sum
Ftotal = 1/(r)2 + 1/(r+d)2 + ... + 1/(r+nd)2
and this sum converges!

There's a lot of questions to ask. How would one calculate a particle's force? These evenly-spaced lines almost remind me of waves. Could we divide space into an infinitesmal grid, give each grid square a potential, and then use something similar to the heat equation to update each square's potential based on its neighbors? In that model, maybe the portal's surgery could be represented by changing which squares are neighbors? I'm not sure!
So it seems I've done surgery on ℝ2, defined a new metric, and used something like the gradient of a function to define a conservative force. My question is: what did I just do? Is there any terminology for what I've done? Any things to learn about to figure out how better to define this maneuver? This feels very topological, and I can't have been the first one to think about vector fields on manifolds. How would I define it rigorously?
