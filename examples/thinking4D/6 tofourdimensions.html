Finally, let's talk about what you've all been waiting for: four dimensions.

By now, you're probably familiar with this 3-dimensional XYZ coordinate system.
We already know that in an XYZ coordinate system, we can think of each point as a combination of points on three different number lines. Can we do the same thing for four dimensions?
Yup - to make a four-dimensional coordinate system, we can use four numbers instead of three. That's it!
To visualize a 4D shape in our 3D universe, we need some rule that tells us where each 4D point should be placed in 3D space, called a "projection". There's infinitely many ways to do this, but I'll show you two of them.
In an XYZ coordinate system, increasing a point's x coordinate will move that point in the direction of the x axis. Can we add a fourth direction to an XYZ coordinate system to represent a fourth coordinate?
Let's try choosing a random direction to represent our new coordinate axis. Following the theme of "letters at the end of the alphabet", let's call this fourth coordinate "W".
Now we can add our "w axis" to our XYZ coordinate system, making an "XYZW coordinate system".
Don't forget that you aren't seeing a real 4-dimensional XYZW coordinate system - just a 3D projection of it. In four dimensions, all four axes should make a right angle with every other axis, but that's impossible in our lame 3D universe. For us, this imperfection means that changing a point's W coordinate will make it seem like the point's XYZ coordinates are changing too, unfortunately)
Here's how this visualization works: if a point's fourth coordinate goes up, we'll move that point in the direction of our "w axis".
Points with negative W coordinates will be moved in the opposite direction.
If you halve the fourth coordinate, you'll move only half the distance along the "w axis", and so on.
Now we can use this XYZW coordinate system for something more fun: visualizing 4D shapes!
Let's start with some simple patterns. To make a 1-dimensional line, you take a point, make a copy that's moved along some direction, and connect the two.
To make a 2-dimensional square, you take a 1-dimensional line, make a copy in a second direction, and connect the two.
To make a 3-dimensional cube, you take a 2-dimensional square, make a copy in a third direction, and connect the two.
So what happens if we continue the pattern?
Let's continue the pattern into the 4th dimension: to make a 4-dimensional hypercube, you take a 3-dimensional cube, make a copy in a fourth direction, and connect the two.
Looks cool, right?
(If it helps, you can think about it as two cubes with every point connected. I've faded out some of the edges to make it easier to see.)
This type of 4D visualization, where we move points a distance along some direction depending on their fourth coordinate, is called an orthographic projection.
Of course, there was nothing special about the random direction we chose to represent the fourth dimension. If we change the direction we chose, we get different types of orthographic projection. The hypercube isn't moving - it's just the way we're visualizing four dimensions that's changing.
One other type of 4D visualization is perspective projection. In this type of projection, we divide the first three coordinates by the fourth coordinate, so points that are farther away in the fourth dimension seem smaller. If you've seen a drawing of a hypercube, it usually looks exactly like this - because that picture was drawn with perspective projection.
There's many different types of perspective projections; you can make other perspective projections by transforming the coordinates before and after dividing, but I won't talk about that here.
One fun thing that we can do with four-dimensional shapes is rotate them! It's going to look very cool.
But first, let's remind ourselves what three-dimensional rotations are.
One way to describe a rotation is by choosing a 2D plane and rotating around that plane. For example, if we chose the XY plane, a rotation would move the X axis to where Y axis is and back again.
If we do the same rotation on a 4D tesseract, it looks mostly similar to the 3D case. That's because these rotations don't touch the w axis, so the parts of the hypercube that are farther in the fourth dimension mirror the same 3D rotation, just moved along slightly in the direction of the w axis because of their different fourth coordinate.
But if we rotate along a plane which does include the w axis, such as the ZW plane (the plane formed by the z axis and w axes), we get this: a beautiful rotation. Choose a cube in the hypercube and watch its fourth coordinate go up and down as it rotates. I think it's pretty mesmerizing.
