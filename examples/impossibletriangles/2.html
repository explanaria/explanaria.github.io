The Three Successive Squares Problem

Choose a number N, such as 10. Can you find three numbers r, s, t such that:
  • r, s, t are all squares, and
  • r + N = s, s+N = t?

Next, I want to tell you about a seemingly unrelated problem, which will lead us to an easier way to solve the congruent number problem.
Choose your favorite N, such as 10. Can you find three square numbers, such that the difference between each two neighboring square numbers is N?
Fibbonacci, the namesake of the Fibbonacci numbers, studied this problem. As it turns out, if we can find three square numbers each N apart, it means N is a congruent number! Let's see why. Assume we already found these three square numbers. Then we can draw three squares - one with area r, one with area s, and one with area $t$.
If we remove the square representing r, the rest of the area is 2N - the \text{green area} is N, and the \text{blue L-shaped area} has area N, so combined their area is 2N.
Now, we're going to cut this little rectangle...
...and paste it on the side of this shape to make a rectangle. Since we haven't added or removed any area, this rectangle has the same area as the shape before: its area is 2N.
Finally, drawing a diagonal across this rectangle splits it into two right triangles, with area N!
In other words - if we can solve the three successive squares problem for a certain N, this triangle shows N is a congruent number!
Just to check, we can also calculate the sides of this triangle - and if you do the math, because r, s, and t are square numbers, we're guaranteed that all the sides are rational numbers.
Great - so if we solve the three successive squares problem, we solve the congruent number problem. Let's start using some algebra to simplify the three successive squares problem!
It's a math fact that if you multiply square numbers together, you get another square number.
If we try to solve the three successive squares problem, we need to find three square numbers r, s, and t. Let's try multiplying r, s, and t together and seeing if we can simplify the equation.
When we multiply together our three square numbers, r, s, and t, we'll get another square number, which I'll call y^2.
We can simplify this with some substitution. Our square numbersr, s, t are all related - once we choose r=s+N and t=s+N.
To reduce the number of variables, let's get rid of r and t by writing them in terms of s: since all three square numbers are N away, we can substitute in r = s-N and t = s+N. Once we substitute into the equation and simplify, we get the equation y^2 = x^3 - N^2 s. Great - now we've reduced our problem to studying solutions of one equation! Finally, let's rename s to x, giving us the equation y^2 = x^3 - N^2 x. That's it for the algebra!
To recap: we've turned the problem of "is N a congruent number?" into "Does y^2 = x^3 - N^2 x have any rational solutions"? The equation y^2 = x^3 - N^2 x is an example of an "elliptic curve". Elliptic curves are very useful. But what we're interested in is solutions: every single pair of rational numbers (x,y) for which y^2 = x^3 - N^2 x.
