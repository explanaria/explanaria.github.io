Inspired by the sphere, we can study more complicated surfaces, like this bagel shape (called a "torus") using multiple 2D coordinate systems.
If we ever reach a dead end, we can make a new coordinate system by clicking the button above. Try moving all the way around the surface of this torus - you'll see that just by moving in a straight line, you can return to your starting point!
And this strategy even works on incredibly complicated surfaces, like this one! The key idea: use Cartesian coordinate systems, which we know how to work with, to study more complicated shapes, even if we don't know how to work with.
A "manifold" is what mathematicians call something that uses this strategy. A manifold has three parts: the space you want to study
(here this mammoth)
, a ton of Cartesian coordinate systems called "charts"
(here 2D ones; the name comes from those real-life paper maps)
, and "transition functions" which tell you how to change from one coordinate system to another
(here, when you drag the point and it appears on a different chart, your device is calculating the transition functions automatically)
.
